01. The Author

Nic Stone was born and raised in Atlanta, Georgia. Before she became a successful author, she worked in teen mentoring. This experience helped her understand the real problems that young people face today.

She wrote Dear Martin because she was frustrated. She saw that many unarmed Black boys were being killed, and she wanted to explore this pain through a story.

Her Motivation:
Nic Stone has two sons. She wrote this book for them. She wanted to answer a difficult question: "What would Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. say if he were alive today?"

The book was published in 2017 and immediately became a bestseller because it felt very real and urgent.

02. Plot Analysis

The Inciting Incident

The story introduces Justyce McAllister, a 17-year-old student. He is the top of his class at Braselton Preparatory Academy. One night, he tries to drive his ex-girlfriend home because she is drunk. A police officer sees them. The officer assumes Justyce is a criminal because he is Black. The officer handcuffs him violently. Justyce is released later, but this trauma changes him.

The Experiment

Justyce is confused. He wants to be a "good citizen," but society sees him as a threat. He decides to start an experiment: "Be Like Martin." He writes letters to Dr. King in his diary. He tries to handle his anger peacefully. However, he faces racism at school from his classmate Jared, and he feels distant from his friends in his neighborhood.

The Turning Point

One day, Justyce and his best friend Manny are driving and listening to loud music. A white man in another car gets angry and yells at them. The situation escalates quickly. This tragic event forces Justyce to question everything. Can peaceful protest really work? Or is the world too broken?

03. Character Profiles

Justyce McAllister

Role: Protagonist
He is an Ivy League applicant (Yale). He feels "split" between two worlds: his rough neighborhood and his rich, mostly white private school. He is analytical, sensitive, and brave.

Manny Rivers

Role: Best Friend
Manny comes from a wealthy Black family. Unlike Justyce, Manny often ignores racism to keep the peace. He stays friends with people who make bad jokes, until he can't take it anymore.

04. Major Themes

Systemic Racism

The book shows that racism is not just about individuals being mean. It is about how the police, the media, and schools treat Black people differently. Justyce follows all the rules, but he is still treated like a criminal.

Identity & Belonging

Justyce feels like he doesn't fit anywhere. His friends in the neighborhood call him a "sellout" because he goes to a private school. His classmates at school see him as an outsider. He has to learn to define himself.
